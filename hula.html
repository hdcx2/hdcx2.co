The Art of Hula

---------

One of the most captivating aspects of Hawaiian culture is hula, the traditional dance that tells stories through movement, chant (oli), and song (mele).
Hula is more than just a dance; it’s a way to pass down history, legends, and values from generation to generation.

Imagine attending a hula performance, where dancers gracefully sway to the rhythm of traditional instruments like the pahu (drum) and ipu (gourd).
There are two main types of hula:

Hula Kahiko: the ancient form accompanied by chanting and percussion

Hula ʻAuana: the modern style that incorporates Western influences and instruments like the ukulele.

The Art of Storytelling Through Hula

Chants (Mele)
Mele are the heart of hula storytelling. These are chants or songs that narrate the stories being depicted in the dance. The words in the mele provide the context and content for the performance, guiding the dancers' movements and expressions.
Poetic Language: Hawaiian mele are often rich in metaphor and imagery, drawing upon natural elements, emotions, and cultural themes to convey deeper meanings. For example, references to the ocean might symbolize change or emotion, while mountains might represent strength and stability.

Types of Mele:
Mele Inoa: Name chants that honor individuals or deities.
Mele Pule: Prayer chants seeking divine guidance or blessings.
Mele Hula: Specific chants created for hula performances, often telling stories of love, nature, and historical events.

Dance Movements
Gestures: Every hand gesture, foot movement, and expression in hula has specific meanings and corresponds to the words in the mele. For example, hand movements might mimic the waves of the sea, represent the leaves of a tree, or symbolize the flight of a bird.
Body Language: The dancer's posture and movements convey emotions and actions, helping to bring the story to life. Graceful movements can depict love and tenderness, while more vigorous motions can convey battles or the power of nature.
Synchronization: The synchronization of dancers, particularly in group performances, enhances the storytelling aspect by creating a unified visual narrative.

Themes in Hula Storytelling
Mythology and Legends: Many hula performances tell stories from Hawaiian mythology, featuring gods and goddesses like Pele, Laka, and Hiʻiaka. These stories explain the creation of the islands, the power of nature, and the adventures of divine beings.
Historical Events: Hula is used to recount significant historical events, such as battles, migrations, and the deeds of ancient chiefs. These stories preserve the cultural heritage and history of the Hawaiian people.
Nature and Environment: The natural world is a central theme in hula, reflecting Hawaii's deep connection to the land and sea. Dances often depict elements like rain, wind, volcanoes, and animals, illustrating their importance in Hawaiian life.
Human Emotions and Relationships: Hula also explores themes of love, friendship, loss, and joy, highlighting universal human experiences and emotions.

Hula Dance

Origins and History

Ancient Beginnings: Hula dates back to ancient Hawaii and was initially performed as a sacred ritual to honor deities, praise chiefs, and recount legends and historical events. It was an essential part of religious and cultural ceremonies.
Mythical Origins: According to Hawaiian mythology, the first hula was performed by the goddess Laka or Hi‘iaka, sister of Pele, the goddess of volcanoes. Laka is often regarded as the goddess of hula and is honored in performances and ceremonies.
Evolution Over Time: While originally performed as a form of worship and storytelling, hula evolved to include entertainment and social gatherings. The dance was influenced by Western contact, leading to changes in style and presentation.

Types of Hula

Hula is generally categorized into two main types, each with distinct characteristics:

Hula Kahiko (Traditional Hula)

Description: Hula Kahiko is the ancient form of hula, characterized by its traditional chants (oli), percussion instruments, and authentic Hawaiian attire. It is more formal and ritualistic.
Costume: Dancers typically wear pa’u skirts made from natural fibers, leis, and other adornments made from native plants. Men might wear malo (loincloths).
Music and Instruments: The chants are often accompanied by traditional instruments like the ipu (gourd drum), pahu (sharkskin drum), and uliuli (feathered gourd rattle).
Themes: The dances focus on historical events, mythology, and honoring the land and deities.

Hula ‘Auana (Modern Hula)

Description: Hula ‘Auana is the more contemporary form of hula, influenced by Western music and instruments. It is known for its flowing movements and melodic tunes.
Costume: Costumes are often more colorful and elaborate, including dresses with floral patterns and more modern accessories.
Music and Instruments: Modern hula incorporates the ukulele, guitar, and other Western instruments. The music is melodic, and the lyrics often focus on love, nature, and modern Hawaiian life.
Themes: This form of hula emphasizes storytelling related to contemporary life, nature, and human emotions.

Elements of Hula

Dance Movements: Hula is known for its graceful movements that include hand gestures, footwork, and hip motions. Each gesture has a specific meaning, conveying the story and emotions of the performance.
Chants and Songs (Mele): The lyrics or chants are integral to hula, as they narrate the story. The dancer’s movements align with the words to create a cohesive and expressive performance.
Costumes and Adornments: Costumes play a significant role in hula, often crafted from natural materials like ti leaves, flowers, and feathers. Each element of the costume can hold symbolic significance.
Cultural Protocol: Performances often begin with a chant or prayer to invoke protection and guidance from Laka and the ancestors. Respecting cultural protocols and traditions is paramount.

Cultural Significance
Storytelling: Hula is a powerful storytelling medium, preserving Hawaiian history, mythology, and culture. It communicates stories of creation, heroism, and the natural world.
Spiritual Connection: Hula is seen as a way to connect with the divine, ancestors, and the spiritual essence of Hawaii. It embodies the spirit of aloha and serves as a form of prayer and meditation.

Cultural Identity: Hula is an expression of Hawaiian identity and pride, representing the resilience and beauty of Hawaiian culture. It is celebrated in festivals, competitions, and cultural events worldwide.
Education and Preservation: Hula is an educational tool that teaches language, history, and cultural values. Efforts to preserve and promote hula are crucial in maintaining Hawaiian cultural heritage.

Learning and Practicing Hula Hālau Hula: Hula schools, known as hālau, are where students learn the art of hula under the guidance of a kumu hula (hula teacher). The kumu hula is responsible for preserving traditions and passing on knowledge. Kumu Hula: The teacher or leader of the hālau hula is highly respected and plays a pivotal role in guiding students in both technique and cultural understanding.
Training and Dedication: Learning hula requires dedication and respect for cultural practices. Students are taught to appreciate the spiritual and historical aspects of the dance. Festivals and Competitions

Merrie Monarch Festival: Held annually in Hilo, Hawaii, the Merrie Monarch Festival is the most prestigious hula competition. It celebrates King David Kalākaua, known as the “Merrie Monarch,” who played a vital role in reviving hula in the 19th century.
Local and International Events: Various festivals and competitions worldwide celebrate hula, promoting cultural exchange and appreciation for Hawaiian arts.
